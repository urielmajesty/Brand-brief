Section 02 — Audience & Market

Who are you talking to?

A brand is a promise made to a specific person. The more precisely we know who that person is, the stronger the brand becomes.

Age range, gender, location, lifestyle, income, values, habits. Do not say "everyone" — specificity is power.

Think beyond functional — what emotional outcome do they get? What feeling or result are they really after?

Your positioning. It doesn't need to be entirely unique — it needs to be believably, authentically yours.

Section 03 — Brand Personality

If your brand were a person…

Personality drives every visual and verbal decision. This is where most briefs fall short — and where great brands are actually born.

How do they dress? How do they speak? What room do they walk into and how does the energy shift? This is the soul of your logo brief.

Select everything that feels true. These guide tone, typography, and visual language.

Section 04 — Visual Language

What should this brand look like?

Visual preferences are symptoms of deeper values. We follow the feeling, not just the colour. Be honest — even rough impressions are useful.

List colors, moods, hex codes if you have them. Note which are preferences vs. hard requirements.

Cross-industry references produce better design thinking. Note what specifically draws you to each one.

Knowing what to avoid is just as valuable as knowing what to pursue.

Section 06 — Final Notes

Anything else I should know?

The best briefs go beyond the obvious. This is your space for context that doesn't fit neatly into a category.

Vision informs scale. A logo for a local business should feel different from one built for national or global expansion.

Heritage, a founder's story, symbolism, language, mythology — these are what separate marks from meaning.

This sets the criteria by which we'll both measure the outcome. Be specific and honest.
